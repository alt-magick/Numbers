The Truth About Math  By Corey White  Introduction: The Unsettling
Idea  It began with a simple, almost absurd question: What if numbers
aren't as absolute as we think?  At first glance, the idea feels
nonsensical. One is always one, two is always two, and mathematical rules are
immutable, eternal, and universal—or so we believe. Numbers are treated as the
bedrock of logic and certainty. They are supposed to be objective,
independent of human thought or language.  Yet the deeper one contemplates the
nature of symbols and meaning, the more that certainty begins to wobble.
What if numbers are not innate truths but constructs of human cognition?
What if they are tools, conventions, or agreements, rather than
self-evident realities?  This essay documents an experiment that challenges these
foundational assumptions: a system where English letters carry numeric values,
where words like "one," "two," and "three" literally sum to the quantities
they represent, and where mathematics and language merge into a single
symbolic universe. What emerges is not merely a curiosity but a profound
exploration of consciousness, meaning, and the nature of reality itself.  Part
I: Historical Context and Philosophical Foundations  Numbers Across
Cultures  Humans have been fascinated by numbers for millennia, yet different
cultures have understood them in radically different ways:  Babylonian
Mathematics: The Babylonians used a sexagesimal (base-60) system. Their numbers
were functional, adapted to their needs in astronomy and commerce, and
very different from our decimal system. The "truth" of a number was
context-dependent.  Pythagoreans (c. 500 BCE): For them, numbers were mystical. "All is
number," they claimed, suggesting that the cosmos itself could be expressed
numerically. Yet their vision linked numbers to patterns, ratios, and even moral
harmony, rather than pure abstraction.  Kabbalah: In Jewish mysticism, each
Hebrew letter carries a numeric value. Words are not merely words—they
encode hidden truths, cosmic principles, and spiritual patterns through
arithmetic. For example, the word chai (life) sums to 18, which is considered
auspicious. Numbers and letters, language and arithmetic, are intertwined. 
Modern mathematics, by contrast, often treats numbers as abstract entities
defined axiomatically. But Kabbalah reminds us that symbols can carry dual
significance—both logical and mystical. Could this duality apply to modern languages
like English?  The Language Experiment Begins  Curiosity prompted a
thought experiment: what if we could assign numeric values to English
letters such that the words for numbers—one, two, three—literally sum to the
quantities they represent?  This is not entirely arbitrary. If each letter has
a unique numeric value, then the sum of letters in one could equal 1,
two could equal 2, and so on. What would such a mapping reveal about the
interplay between language and arithmetic? Could the symbolic structure of
English words secretly encode arithmetic relationships?  I wrote a simple
program to search for possible letter-to-number mappings. To my surprise,
consistent mappings exist. This suggested something astonishing: the English
language, often dismissed as arbitrary and messy, contains hidden numeric
possibilities waiting to be uncovered.  Part II: Building the System  Defining the
Problem  At the heart of the experiment lies a simple question: Can we
assign each letter a numeric value so that the sum of letters in each
number word equals the number itself?  For example:  one → O + N + E = 1 
two → T + W + O = 2  three → T + H + R + E + E = 3  The challenge is to
find a consistent mapping of letters to numbers.  Constraints and
Considerations:  Uniqueness: Each letter is assigned exactly one value. 
Consistency: The same letter must have the same value wherever it appears. 
Completeness: The mapping should work for all basic English number words (1–10
initially).  Flexibility: Both positive and negative integers can be used to
allow a solution space.  This transforms the problem into a type of linear
algebra equation system: each word is an equation, and each letter is a
variable.  Representing the System Mathematically  We start by identifying all
letters used in English number words from one to ten:  O, N, E, T, W, H, R,
F, U, I, V, S, X, G  Each word produces an equation:  one → O + N + E =

= 9  ten → T + E + N = 10  We now have 10 equations and 16 unknowns
(letters). This is an underdetermined system—meaning there are multiple
possible solutions.  Solving the System  To solve, I wrote a computer program
using a backtracking algorithm:  Assign trial integer values to letters. 
Check if each word equation sums to its numeric value.  If an equation
fails, backtrack and try a different assignment.  Repeat until a consistent
set of values satisfies all equations.  The program successfully found

Numbers Are Not Absolute  The letter-number system demonstrates that numbers
may not be universal, objective truths:  The values assigned to letters
are arbitrary yet consistent.  The sum of letters produces the intended
numeric result, but only within the rules we define.  Change the letter
assignments, and the numeric outcomes shift, creating alternate valid universes
of arithmetic.  Implication: Numbers may not exist independently "out
there"; they could be constructs of the mind, stabilized by agreed-upon
symbols. Mathematics, often considered the ultimate objective truth, may
instead be a framework of coherent relationships, like a language we
collectively read and interpret.  Language and Mathematics as Interconnected
Systems  Both language and mathematics operate on symbols and rules, yet we
often treat them separately. The letter-number system demonstrates that
these are not separate domains—they are two dialects of the same symbolic
universe.  Three + five = eight aligns both linguistically (the word "eight")
and mathematically (3 + 5 = 8). Letters carry semantic weight (the
meaning of the word) and numeric weight (the arithmetic sum), uniting
language and math.  Multiple Forms of Truth  A surprising outcome of this
system is the existence of multiple valid mappings. Each letter can
potentially have several assignments that satisfy the equations. Each mapping
represents a different but internally consistent universe.  Philosophical
Reflection: Truth may not be singular; it may be context-dependent, emerging
from the relationships within a system. Just as quantum mechanics allows
multiple simultaneous possibilities until observed, symbolic systems may
harbor latent truths that only become meaningful when interpreted.  The
Bridge Between Mysticism and Logic  The system mirrors aspects of mystical
traditions. Kabbalah assigns numerical values to Hebrew letters, creating hidden
relationships between words and numbers. Our letter-number mapping replicates this
principle in English: hidden arithmetic emerges from words themselves.  The
system is logically sound: equations balance, exponentiation works, and
complex arithmetic is valid. Simultaneously, it evokes wonder: letters
"become" numbers, words "contain" arithmetic, and meaning emerges from
symbolic interplay.  This demonstrates that logic and mysticism are not
mutually exclusive; rather, they are complementary ways of uncovering hidden
structures in reality.  Relationships Are Fundamental  At its core, the
letter-number system reveals a key principle: symbols derive power from
relationships.  The number "1" has no meaning in isolation; it gains significance
only through contrast with "2" and "3."  The letter "E" contributes
meaning only when combined with other letters in a word.  Emergent Insight:
Truth, meaning, and understanding are relational, not absolute.
Systems—linguistic, mathematical, or even physical—gain coherence and significance
through interaction.  Part V: The Secret Engine of Meaning  Symbols in
Isolation Are Silent  A symbol without context is meaningless:  "A" is just a

 A + B = C  O + N + E = 1  Letters form words, words form sentences,

Philosophical Insight: Consciousness is not merely a passive witness. It is an
active participant in creating meaning, turning symbols into functional

Every equation is a sentence. Every solution is a story. Every observer is
a co-author of symbolic reality.  Part VI: The Multiverse of Meaning

we can imagine an infinite symbolic multiverse:  Each mapping
represents a parallel universe of arithmetic.  Equations remain true within
that universe, even if they contradict other mappings.  Meaning and
mathematical truths are observer-relative, dependent on which symbolic universe
the mind inhabits.  This mirrors ideas in modern physics. Quantum
mechanics shows that multiple potential states exist simultaneously until
observed. Multiverse theory suggests parallel universes operate according to
different laws, yet each is internally consistent. In the same way, symbolic

Implications for Mathematics  The multiverse concept challenges classical
assumptions:  Numbers are not fixed absolutes: They are emergent from symbolic
relationships and rules.  Mathematical truths are relative: A theorem that holds
in one mapping may not hold in another, yet both are internally

"mathematical world" governed by its own rules.  This is reminiscent of Gödel's
incompleteness theorem: Even in a consistent system, some truths exist that cannot
be proven within that system. Similarly, some properties may only
emerge when comparing multiple mappings, revealing meta-truths across
universes.  Part VII: Gödel, Incompleteness, and Symbolic Systems  The Mystery
of Mathematical Truth  Kurt Gödel's work in the early 20th century
fundamentally changed our understanding of mathematics. His Incompleteness
Theorems showed that:  In any consistent, sufficiently powerful formal
system, there exist statements that are true but unprovable within the
system.  The system cannot prove its own consistency.  In other words,
mathematics has intrinsic limitations, no matter how elegant or rigorous the
formal rules.  This resonates deeply with our letter-number experiment.
Both systems reveal the boundaries of symbolic truth and the necessity of
an external observer or interpreter.  Letter-Numbers and Gödel's
Insights  Our experiment assigns numeric values to letters, producing a
self-consistent system. The system works internally, but:  Its validity depends on
the mapping we chose.  Multiple alternative mappings could exist,
creating parallel, consistent symbolic universes.  The rules themselves
cannot account for all possible truths outside the system without an
external interpreter.  This mirrors Gödel's insight: any formal system is
limited. Just as our mapping cannot prove truths about other possible
mappings without stepping outside itself, arithmetic cannot account for all
true statements within its own axioms.  Truth Beyond Formal Rules  Gödel
showed that truth transcends formal derivation. Similarly, in the
letter-number system:  Arithmetic emerges from syntactic choices (letter values). 
Some truths—such as "alternative mappings exist"—require stepping outside
the constructed system.  Observers (like us) are needed to recognize,
interpret, and connect these truths.  This suggests a profound principle:
Formal rules generate structure, but consciousness discovers truth beyond
the structure.  Implications for Creativity and the Mind  Gödel's
incompleteness theorems imply that intuition, imagination, and creativity are
essential in mathematics:  Some truths cannot be derived mechanically.  Humans
(or conscious observers) provide insight that formal rules alone cannot
generate.  Our letter-number system is a microcosm of this. The program
constructs possible mappings, but only a conscious mind can choose, interpret,
and explore the system. Emergent truths appear only when structure meets
perception.  Thus, Gödel reinforces the role of consciousness as a co-creator of
mathematical reality, not merely a passive observer.  Part VIII: Numbers as
